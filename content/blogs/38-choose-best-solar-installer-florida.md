# How to Choose the Best Solar Installer in Florida

**Choosing the best solar installer in Florida means verifying an active EPC license, confirming hurricane-rated mounting hardware, demanding a 25-year production warranty backed by the installer (not just the manufacturer), and ensuring the company uses in-house crews rather than subcontractors. The right choice protects your roof, your savings, and your investment for decades.**

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Licensing is non-negotiable.** Florida requires a valid Electrical or Solar Contractor license (or General Contractor with solar specialty) — always verify through the DBPR before signing anything.
- **Hurricane-rated mounting matters more than panels.** Florida's wind exposure means racking systems must meet local building codes for 150+ mph wind loads; not every installer uses compliant hardware.
- **In-house installation crews outperform subcontractors.** Companies that subcontract labor have less quality control, slower warranty response times, and higher rates of roof-leak callbacks.
- **A 25-year warranty means nothing if the company folds.** Evaluate the installer's financial stability, years in business, and whether warranty obligations transfer if the company is acquired.
- **Transparent financing separates good companies from predatory ones.** Demand a full amortization schedule, confirm the dealer fee, and compare the true cost of a solar loan vs. a lease before you sign.

## Why Your Choice of Solar Installer Matters More in Florida

Florida is one of the fastest-growing residential solar markets in the United States. That growth has attracted hundreds of solar companies — some excellent, many mediocre, and a few that are outright problematic.

The stakes of choosing the wrong solar company in Florida are higher than in most states for three reasons:

1. **Hurricane exposure.** A poorly mounted system can become a projectile in a Category 3+ storm. Installers who cut corners on racking or flashing put your roof and your neighbors at risk.
2. **Aggressive sales culture.** Florida's large retiree population and year-round sunshine make it a magnet for high-pressure solar sales operations that prioritize volume over quality.
3. **Complex permitting landscape.** Each Florida county has its own permitting office, inspection requirements, and utility interconnection process. Inexperienced installers create delays that can stretch a 6-week project into 6 months.

The difference between the best solar installer in Florida and a mediocre one is not the panels on your roof — it is the quality of the installation, the integrity of the warranty, and the transparency of the financing.

## Red Flags That Should Make You Walk Away

Before we cover what to look for, here are warning signs that a solar company in Florida is not worth your time:

### High-Pressure Sales Tactics
If a salesperson tells you the price is "only good today" or that a tax credit is "expiring this month," that is a red flag. The federal solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC) has a published legislative timeline. Any company using false urgency is prioritizing their commission over your decision-making.

### No Physical Office in Florida
Some companies operate entirely through door-to-door canvassers and remote call centers. If you cannot visit a physical office or meet the installation team before signing, proceed with extreme caution.

### Vague Warranty Language
Watch for warranties that say "up to 25 years" or "manufacturer's warranty passthrough." A strong installer stands behind their own workmanship warranty — separate from the panel and inverter manufacturer warranties.

### Unwillingness to Provide References
Any reputable solar installer in Florida should be able to provide at least 10 local references in your county. If they hesitate, ask yourself why.

### Quoting Without a Site Visit or Satellite Assessment
A company that provides a binding quote without assessing your roof condition, shading, electrical panel, and orientation is guessing — and you will pay for those guesses later in change orders.

## 10 Questions to Ask Every Solar Installer Before You Sign

Use this checklist during every sales consultation. A trustworthy solar company will answer all 10 without hesitation.

### 1. What is your Florida contractor license number?
They should provide it immediately. Verify it on the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) website.

### 2. Do you use in-house crews or subcontractors?
This is one of the most important questions you can ask. More on this below.

### 3. What racking system do you use, and is it rated for my county's wind speed requirements?
Florida building code requires racking to meet specific wind-load thresholds based on your location and proximity to the coast.

### 4. What happens if my roof leaks after installation?
The answer should be a clear workmanship warranty that covers roof penetrations — not a referral back to your roofing company.

### 5. Can you walk me through the full financing terms, including the dealer fee?
Many solar loans include a 15-30% dealer fee baked into the loan balance. A transparent company discloses this upfront and explains how it affects your total cost.

### 6. How many installations have you completed in my county?
Local experience matters. An installer with 500 jobs in Miami-Dade knows that county's inspection process far better than one with 500 jobs in Texas and 5 in Florida.

### 7. What is the timeline from contract to Permission to Operate (PTO)?
A realistic answer for most Florida counties is 8-14 weeks. If someone promises 3 weeks, they are either unfamiliar with your utility or being dishonest.

### 8. Do you handle HOA approval?
Florida's Solar Rights Act (FL Statute 163.04) limits how HOAs can restrict solar, but disputes still arise. Experienced installers handle HOA submissions and know how to navigate pushback.

### 9. What monitoring platform do you provide?
You should be able to track your system's production in real time from day one. Ask whether monitoring is included for the life of the system or requires a subscription.

### 10. What is your process for warranty claims?
Ask who handles the claim, what the average response time is, and whether they stock replacement parts locally.

## In-House Crews vs. Subcontractors: Why It Matters

This is one of the most overlooked factors when choosing a solar installer in Florida — and one of the most consequential.

### How the Subcontractor Model Works
Many large solar companies operate as sales organizations. They sell the system, design it, and then hand off the physical installation to a subcontracted crew. That crew may be installing for three or four different solar brands in the same week.

### Why In-House Crews Deliver Better Results

| Factor | In-House Crews | Subcontractors |
|---|---|---|
| **Quality control** | Direct supervision, consistent standards | Variable quality, limited oversight |
| **Accountability** | One company owns the entire process | Finger-pointing between sales co. and sub |
| **Warranty response** | Same team that installed can return to fix | Sub may no longer work with that company |
| **Roof leak liability** | Clear chain of responsibility | Disputes over who is responsible |
| **Communication** | Direct line to the install team | Information filtered through middlemen |

When a company uses its own employees for every installation, the quality control is inherently tighter. The installer's reputation rides on every single roof.

## Hurricane-Rated Installation: What You Need to Know

Florida's building code is among the strictest in the nation for wind resistance, and for good reason. Your solar installation must survive the same storms your roof is rated for.

### What "Hurricane-Rated" Actually Means
There is no single "hurricane-rated" certification for solar panels. Instead, compliance is determined by:

- **Local wind speed requirements.** The Florida Building Code assigns design wind speeds by location. Coastal areas (especially Miami-Dade and Broward counties) require systems rated for 170+ mph winds.
- **Racking and attachment engineering.** The mounting system must be engineered for your specific roof type, pitch, and wind exposure category. This requires a stamped engineering letter from a licensed Florida PE.
- **Proper flashing and waterproofing.** Every roof penetration must be sealed with code-compliant flashing. This is where most installation failures occur — and where subcontractor quality variance is most dangerous.

### Questions to Ask About Wind Resistance
- What racking brand do you use, and what is its maximum wind rating?
- Will a licensed Florida PE stamp the engineering plans for my specific roof?
- How do you flash and seal each roof penetration?
- Does your workmanship warranty explicitly cover storm-related installation failures?

If an installer cannot answer these questions in detail, they should not be on your roof in the Sunshine State.

## Warranty Comparison: What to Actually Look For

Solar warranties are not created equal. Here is how to compare them like an informed buyer.

### The Three Warranties That Matter

**1. Panel Manufacturer Product Warranty (typically 25 years)**
Covers defects in the solar panels themselves. This comes from the panel manufacturer (e.g., REC, Qcells, Canadian Solar), not from your installer.

**2. Inverter Warranty (typically 12-25 years)**
Covers the inverter or microinverters. Enphase microinverters carry a 25-year warranty; some string inverters only carry 12 years.

**3. Installer Workmanship Warranty (varies widely: 2-25 years)**
This is the warranty that separates a good solar company from a great one. It covers the quality of the installation itself: the racking, the wiring, the roof penetrations, and everything the installer's crew touched.

### The Warranty Red Flags
- **Workmanship warranty under 10 years.** Your panels are warrantied for 25 years. If the installer only stands behind their work for 5, what does that tell you about their confidence in their own crews?
- **No written warranty document.** If the warranty terms are not in your contract, they do not exist.
- **Warranty contingent on annual maintenance purchases.** Some companies void their warranty if you do not buy their maintenance plan. Legitimate workmanship warranties do not come with strings like this.

## Florida Solar Licensing Requirements: How to Verify Your Installer

Florida law requires solar installers to hold one of the following active licenses:

- **Certified Electrical Contractor (EC)** — issued by the state
- **Registered Electrical Contractor** — issued at the county level
- **Certified Solar Contractor** — a specialty license for solar-specific work
- **Certified General Contractor (CGC)** with a qualifying solar sub-specialty

### How to Verify a License
1. Visit the [Florida DBPR License Verification Portal](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp).
2. Search by the company name or license number.
3. Confirm the license is **active** (not expired, suspended, or revoked).
4. Check for any disciplinary actions or complaints.

### Why This Step Is Critical
Unlicensed solar installation is more common than you would think. Some companies operate under a single license holder who "qualifies" dozens of subcontracted crews — a practice that creates liability gaps if something goes wrong.

An installer with a clean, active Florida license and a history free of DBPR complaints is a baseline requirement, not a bonus.

## Comparing Solar Quotes: Beyond the Bottom Line

When you have three or four quotes from different solar companies in Florida, the lowest price is almost never the right way to choose. Here is how to compare apples to apples.

### What Every Quote Should Include
- **System size (kW)** and estimated annual production (kWh)
- **Panel brand and model** — not just "Tier 1 panels"
- **Inverter brand and model** — string inverter vs. microinverters
- **Racking brand** and wind load rating
- **Total gross cost** before incentives
- **Federal ITC amount** (currently 30% through 2032)
- **Net cost** after incentives
- **Financing terms** including interest rate, term length, and dealer fee
- **True cost of electricity** — the $/kWh you will actually pay over the loan term

### The Dealer Fee Trap
Many solar loans advertise a low interest rate (1.49%-3.99%) but include a dealer fee of 15-30% that is rolled into your loan balance. A $30,000 system with a 25% dealer fee means you are actually financing $37,500.

A transparent solar company will show you both the cash price and the financed price, and explain the difference. If a salesperson cannot clearly articulate the dealer fee, that is a disqualifying red flag.

### Cost Per Watt Is Your Best Comparison Tool
Divide the total net cost by the system size in watts. In Florida's current market, a fair installed price for a quality residential system ranges from $2.50-$3.25 per watt before incentives. Prices significantly below this range should prompt questions about equipment quality or hidden fees.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What license does a solar installer need in Florida?
A solar installer in Florida must hold a valid Certified Electrical Contractor (EC), Certified Solar Contractor, or Certified General Contractor license with solar qualification. You can verify any license through the Florida DBPR portal at myfloridalicense.com. Never hire an installer who cannot provide their license number immediately.

### How do I know if solar panels are hurricane-rated in Florida?
There is no single "hurricane-rated" label. Instead, your solar installation must meet Florida Building Code wind-load requirements for your specific location. Coastal areas require systems engineered for 170+ mph winds. Ask your installer for a stamped engineering letter from a licensed Florida Professional Engineer.

### Should I choose a local or national solar company in Florida?
Local solar companies typically offer faster response times, deeper knowledge of county permitting processes, and stronger accountability. National companies may offer lower prices but often subcontract installation to local crews, which reduces quality control. Prioritize companies with in-house crews and a physical presence in your area.

### What is the average cost of solar installation in Florida?
As of 2025, the average cost of residential solar in Florida ranges from $2.50 to $3.25 per watt before the 30% federal tax credit. For a typical 8-10 kW system, that means a gross cost of $20,000-$32,500 and a net cost of $14,000-$22,750 after the ITC. Always compare net cost per watt across quotes.

### What warranty should I expect from a Florida solar installer?
Look for three separate warranties: a 25-year panel manufacturer product warranty, a 25-year inverter warranty (standard with microinverters like Enphase), and a minimum 10-year installer workmanship warranty — though the best companies, including RIV Solar, offer 25-year workmanship coverage that matches the equipment lifespan.
