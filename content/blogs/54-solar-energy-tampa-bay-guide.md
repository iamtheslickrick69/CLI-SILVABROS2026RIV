## TECO vs Duke Energy: What Solar Customers Need to Know

Tampa Bay is split between two major utilities, and understanding which one serves your home is an important first step in the solar process.

### Tampa Electric (TECO)

TECO serves most of Hillsborough County, including Tampa, Brandon, Riverview, Valrico, and parts of Plant City. TECO has been relatively supportive of residential solar, offering straightforward interconnection and net metering agreements.

Key details for TECO solar customers:

- **Net metering available** for systems up to 2 MW
- **Excess generation credits** roll forward monthly at the full retail rate
- **Annual true-up** settles any remaining credits, typically at an avoided-cost rate
- **No additional solar surcharges** on residential accounts
- **Interconnection approval** typically takes 2 to 4 weeks after installation

### Duke Energy (Florida)

Duke Energy serves most of Pinellas County, including St. Petersburg, Clearwater, Largo, Seminole, and parts of Pasco County. Duke's net metering program is also favorable for solar customers, though the process and rate structure differ slightly.

Key details for Duke Energy solar customers:

- **Net metering available** under their solar self-generation program
- **Excess generation credited** at the retail rate on a monthly basis
- **Slightly different interconnection timeline** — plan for 3 to 6 weeks
- **No demand charges** for standard residential solar accounts
- **Duke's tiered rate structure** means high-usage homes benefit even more from solar

### Which Utility Is Better for Solar?

Both TECO and Duke Energy offer solid net metering programs. The real difference comes down to your specific rate plan, usage patterns, and home location. ## Net Metering by Utility in Tampa Bay

Net metering is the policy that makes solar financially powerful. When your solar panels produce more electricity than your home uses during the day, that excess energy flows back to the grid and your utility gives you a credit.

### How Net Metering Works with TECO

With TECO, net metering credits accumulate monthly. If your system produces more than you use in March, those excess credits carry forward to April. At the end of your annual billing cycle, any remaining credits are settled — usually at a lower avoided-cost rate. This means the smartest strategy is to size your system so monthly production closely matches annual consumption, minimizing leftover credits at true-up.

### How Net Metering Works with Duke Energy

Duke Energy's net metering operates similarly but with its own nuances. Credits roll forward month to month, and Duke's tiered pricing means that solar offsets the most expensive kilowatt-hours first. Homes with higher energy usage often see a faster payback period with Duke because those top-tier rates are the first to be eliminated.

### Maximizing Your Net Metering Value

Regardless of your utility, the goal is to design a system that maximizes self-consumption and credits during peak production months (March through October) to offset the lower-production winter months. ## Lightning, Storms, and Solar: What Tampa Bay Homeowners Should Know

Tampa Bay is famously known as the lightning capital of the United States. Between June and September, afternoon thunderstorms are a near-daily occurrence. Naturally, homeowners ask: what happens to solar panels during lightning and storms?

### Solar Panels and Lightning

Modern solar installations include grounding systems that protect against lightning strikes. Your solar array is connected to your home's electrical grounding, and inverters include surge protection. Direct lightning strikes to solar panels are extremely rare — panels are low-profile and no more likely to attract lightning than any other part of your roof.

### Hurricane Readiness

Solar panels installed to Florida Building Code are rated for wind speeds up to 160 mph or higher. RIV Solar's in-house installation crews follow all FBC requirements, using engineered mounting systems and proper flashing to maintain roof integrity. In many cases, solar panels actually protect the roofing material underneath from wind and hail damage.

### Battery Backup for Storm Season

This is where Tampa Bay homeowners gain a significant advantage. A solar-plus-battery system keeps your lights, refrigerator, and essential circuits running during power outages — which happen frequently during summer storms and can last days after a hurricane. With battery storage, your home becomes its own power source when the grid goes down. Given Tampa Bay's storm exposure, battery backup is not a luxury; it is a practical investment.

Tampa Bay's geography spans from Gulf Coast beaches to inland suburbs, and solar system design should account for these differences.

### Coastal Installations (St. Pete, Clearwater, Indian Rocks Beach, Treasure Island)

Homes within a few miles of the coast face salt air exposure, which can accelerate corrosion on lower-quality mounting hardware. RIV Solar uses marine-grade, anodized aluminum racking and stainless steel fasteners for all coastal installations. We also ensure panel micro-inverter or optimizer connections are properly sealed against moisture intrusion.

Coastal homes also face higher wind loads during hurricanes. Our engineering team accounts for these loads in every system design, ensuring compliance with the Florida Building Code's high-velocity hurricane zone requirements where applicable.

### Inland Installations (Brandon, Riverview, Wesley Chapel, Plant City)

Inland homes face fewer corrosion concerns but often have larger roof areas and higher energy consumption due to bigger square footage. These homes are ideal candidates for larger systems (10 kW+) that can fully offset utility bills. Tree shading is more common inland, so a proper shade analysis is essential before installation.

## Local Incentives and Savings in Tampa Bay

Tampa Bay homeowners benefit from a strong stack of federal, state, and local incentives that make solar more affordable than most people realize.

### Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC)

The 30% federal tax credit remains available through 2032. For a $30,000 solar installation, that is a $9,000 credit applied directly to your federal tax liability. If you add battery storage, the battery qualifies for the same 30% credit.

### Florida Property Tax Exemption

Florida law exempts the added value of a solar system from your property tax assessment. Your home value goes up, but your property taxes do not. In Tampa Bay's appreciating real estate market, this makes solar an even stronger investment.

### Florida Sales Tax Exemption

Solar equipment is exempt from Florida's 6% sales tax (plus any county surtax). On a $30,000 system, that saves you $1,800 or more — a benefit you receive automatically at purchase.

### Net Metering Savings

As detailed above, net metering with TECO or Duke Energy can offset 90% to 100% of your electricity bill. Combined with the federal tax credit and Florida's exemptions, most Tampa Bay homeowners see a payback period of 7 to 10 years — with 15 to 18 additional years of essentially free electricity under warranty.
