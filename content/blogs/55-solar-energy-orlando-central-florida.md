## OUC vs. Duke Energy: Understanding Your Utility Provider

One of the first things Orlando-area homeowners need to understand before going solar is which utility provider serves their address. The two primary providers in the Central Florida region — **Orlando Utilities Commission (OUC)** and **Duke Energy Florida** — have different solar programs, rate structures, and net metering policies.

### OUC (Orlando Utilities Commission)

OUC is a municipal utility serving the City of Orlando and parts of unincorporated Orange County and Osceola County. Here is what solar homeowners need to know about OUC:

- **Net metering:** OUC offers a net metering program that credits solar customers for excess energy sent back to the grid. Credits are applied at the retail rate, though OUC periodically reviews and adjusts its program terms.
- **OUC solar programs:** OUC has historically offered its own community solar and rooftop solar programs. However, homeowner-owned solar systems typically provide better long-term value than utility-managed programs because you own the asset and capture the full financial benefit.
- **Interconnection process:** OUC has a defined interconnection process for residential solar. A qualified installer handles the application, permitting, and inspection coordination on your behalf.

### Duke Energy Florida

Duke Energy serves a large portion of Central Florida outside of OUC's territory, including areas of Orange County, Seminole County, Lake County, Osceola County, and Polk County. Key details for Duke Energy solar customers:

- **Net metering:** Duke Energy Florida offers net metering that credits solar customers for excess generation. Credits roll over month to month, which is particularly valuable during high-production summer months when you may generate more than you consume.
- **Rate structure:** Duke Energy's tiered rate structure means that homeowners who consume more electricity see progressively higher per-kWh charges. Solar energy offsets the most expensive kilowatt-hours first, maximizing savings.
- **Grid services charge:** Duke Energy applies a monthly grid access charge to solar customers. While this is a cost to factor in, the overall savings from solar production typically far exceed this fee.

### Which Utility Do I Have?

If you live within Orlando city limits, you are most likely served by OUC. If you live in the surrounding suburbs — particularly in **Clermont, Ocoee, Winter Park, Sanford, Kissimmee (outside certain zones), or unincorporated Lake or Seminole County** — you are likely a Duke Energy customer. Your monthly electric bill will clearly show your provider.

## Net Metering in Central Florida: How It Works

Net metering is the mechanism that makes residential solar financially powerful. Here is the straightforward explanation:

When your solar panels produce more electricity than your home is using at any given moment, that excess energy flows back into the grid. Your utility meter literally runs backward (or registers a credit on a smart meter). At night or during periods of heavy consumption, you draw electricity from the grid as usual.

At the end of each billing cycle, you only pay for the **net** difference between what you consumed from the grid and what you sent back. In many months — particularly spring and fall when production is high and air conditioning use is moderate — Central Florida solar homeowners end up with a net credit on their bill.

### Maximizing Net Metering in Orlando

To get the most from net metering in the Orlando area:

1. **Right-size your system.** A properly designed solar system should offset 90-110% of your annual electricity consumption. Oversizing beyond what net metering credits can offset wastes your investment.
2. **Consider time-of-use patterns.** Running major appliances (dishwasher, laundry, pool pump) during peak solar production hours means you consume your own solar power directly rather than sending it to the grid and buying it back later.
3. **Add battery storage.** A home battery system lets you store excess solar energy for use during evening peak hours or during power outages, reducing your grid dependence even further.

## Hurricane Preparedness: Why Battery Backup Matters in Orlando

Central Florida sits squarely in hurricane territory. The region has experienced direct impacts from major storms — **Hurricane Charley (2004)** tore through the I-4 corridor, and **Hurricane Ian (2022)** caused widespread damage and extended power outages across Orange, Osceola, and Seminole counties.

After Hurricane Ian, some Orlando-area residents waited **one to two weeks** for power restoration. For families with medical equipment, elderly household members, or young children, extended outages are not just inconvenient — they are dangerous.

### Solar Panels Alone Are Not Enough

Here is a critical detail many homeowners do not realize: **standard grid-tied solar panel systems shut down during a power outage.** This is a safety requirement (called anti-islanding) designed to protect utility workers repairing power lines. Without a battery, your solar panels will not power your home during an outage even if the sun is shining.

### Solar + Battery = True Energy Independence

Adding a home battery system — such as the Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ Battery, or similar technology — changes the equation entirely. During an outage:

- Your solar panels continue generating electricity.
- That electricity charges your battery.
- Your battery powers your home's essential circuits (refrigerator, lights, Wi-Fi, medical devices, phone charging).
- You can sustain this cycle for days, even weeks, as long as the sun continues to shine.

For Orlando and Central Florida homeowners, **solar plus battery storage is not a luxury — it is a practical necessity.** RIV Solar designs every battery backup system to prioritize the circuits that matter most to your household, and our **25-year warranty** covers both panels and associated equipment.

## Solar Incentives Available to Orlando Homeowners

Central Florida homeowners benefit from a strong combination of federal and state incentives that significantly reduce the cost of going solar:

### Federal Solar Investment Tax Credit (ITC) — 30%

The federal ITC allows you to claim **30% of your total solar system cost** as a dollar-for-dollar credit on your federal income taxes. For a $25,000 system, that is a $7,500 tax credit. This incentive is available through 2032, after which it begins to step down.

### Florida Property Tax Exemption

Florida law exempts the added value of a solar energy system from your property tax assessment. This means your solar panels increase your home's market value without increasing your property taxes — a significant financial advantage.

### Florida Sales Tax Exemption

The purchase of a solar energy system in Florida is exempt from the state's 6% sales tax. On a $25,000 system, that is an immediate $1,500 savings at the point of purchase.

### Net Metering Credits

As discussed above, net metering from OUC or Duke Energy provides ongoing monthly savings by crediting you for excess solar energy sent back to the grid.
