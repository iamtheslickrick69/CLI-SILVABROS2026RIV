## The Puerto Rico Solar Landscape in 2026

Puerto Rico is one of the most compelling residential solar markets in the United States — and one of the most complicated to navigate as a homeowner.

The fundamentals are extraordinary. LUMA Energy rates regularly exceed $0.30 per kWh and continue climbing. The island receives over 5.5 peak sun hours per day, placing it among the best solar resources in any U.S. jurisdiction. Grid reliability remains a persistent problem, with outages that range from inconvenient to dangerous. And local tax exemptions — including an 11.5% sales tax exemption on solar equipment and a 100% property tax exemption on added home value — make the financial case even stronger.

These conditions have created a solar boom. Since Hurricane Maria in 2017 and the subsequent grid failures under PREPA and now LUMA, thousands of homeowners have gone solar. The market has attracted dozens of solar companies — national brands, regional specialists, local startups, and mainland operations setting up temporary shop on the island.

That is exactly where the problem starts.

Not all of these companies are equal. Some have been on the island for years, have trained local crews, and have built reputations installation by installation. Others arrived recently, subcontract everything, and may not be around in three years to honor a warranty. A few actively mislead homeowners about the federal tax credit, use high-pressure door-to-door tactics, or quote prices that seem too good because they are.

In a market this active, the single most important decision you make is not whether to go solar — the math on that is clear. The most important decision is which company you trust to do the work.

This guide gives you the specific criteria, red flags, and questions that separate the best solar companies in Puerto Rico from the rest.

## What Makes a Great Solar Company in Puerto Rico

Before comparing individual companies, you need to understand what "great" actually looks like in this market. Puerto Rico is not the mainland. The environmental demands, regulatory landscape, and customer needs are different, and the best solar installers on the island reflect that.

### Deep Local Knowledge

Puerto Rico has its own permitting requirements, its own utility interconnection process through LUMA, its own incentive structure, and its own building code considerations for hurricane-prone construction. A company that installs solar in 15 states may know solar, but they may not know Puerto Rico. The best companies understand the island's specific regulatory environment, have relationships with local authorities, and can navigate LUMA's interconnection process without delays.

### Climate-Appropriate Design and Installation

Every solar installation in Puerto Rico must withstand tropical humidity, salt air corrosion (especially in coastal areas), intense UV exposure, heavy rainfall, and hurricane-force winds. This is not the same as designing a system for a ranch house in Arizona. The best companies in Puerto Rico select equipment rated for these conditions, use hurricane-rated mounting systems, and install with the understanding that their work will be tested by nature in ways that mainland installations rarely face.

### Bilingual Communication

Puerto Rico is a bilingual market. The best solar companies communicate fluently in both English and Spanish — not just in sales, but throughout the entire process: proposal review, contract explanation, installation coordination, warranty claims, and ongoing service. If a company cannot communicate clearly in your preferred language at every stage, that is a limitation that will cost you.

### Financial Stability and Long-Term Commitment

A 25-year warranty is only worth something if the company exists in 25 years. The best solar companies in Puerto Rico have financial stability, a permanent presence on the island, and a demonstrated commitment to the market beyond a single boom cycle. They are not here for a quick run — they are building long-term operations with staff, infrastructure, and reputation at stake.

## Top Criteria to Evaluate Solar Companies in Puerto Rico

When you are comparing solar installers in Puerto Rico in 2026, these are the specific criteria that matter most. Weight them heavily.

### 1. Licensing and Insurance

This is the baseline. Any solar installer must hold the appropriate electrical and contractor licenses for Puerto Rico. They should also carry general liability insurance and workers' compensation coverage. Ask for documentation and verify independently — do not take anyone's word for it.

The rapid growth of the Puerto Rico solar market has attracted some operators who are not fully credentialed. An unlicensed installation can void your homeowner's insurance, create code violations, and leave you without recourse.

### 2. In-House Crews vs. Subcontractors

This is one of the most important distinctions in the entire solar industry, and it directly affects the quality of your installation.

Companies that employ their own installation crews control the training, standards, equipment handling, and accountability of every person on your roof. If something goes wrong, there is one company responsible. No finger-pointing. No runaround.

Companies that subcontract the installation work are outsourcing their most critical function to third parties. The crew that installs your system may work for multiple companies simultaneously. The solar company that sold you the system has limited control over their workmanship, and when an issue arises years later, the subcontractor may no longer work for that company or may no longer exist at all.

In Puerto Rico, where installations must survive hurricane conditions, the quality of workmanship is not a minor detail. Always ask: do you use in-house crews or subcontractors? And verify the answer.

### 3. Warranty Coverage (and the Company Behind It)

"25-year warranty" can mean vastly different things. A comprehensive warranty should cover:

- **Panel performance** (typically guaranteeing 80-85% output after 25 years)
- **Inverter** (12-25 years depending on manufacturer)
- **Battery** (10-15 years for capacity and cycles)
- **Workmanship and labor** (the installation itself — wiring, mounting, connections)
- **Roof penetration** (guaranteeing that mounting hardware entry points will not leak)

Watch for warranties that exclude labor costs. Some companies will replace a defective component under warranty but charge you hundreds of dollars in labor to install it. Watch for warranties from companies with two years of operating history — a 25-year warranty from a startup with no financial backing is a piece of paper, not a guarantee.

### 4. Financing Transparency

Before signing anything, you should have clear, written answers to:

- Total system cost (cash price)
- Total financed cost (principal plus all interest and fees)
- Interest rate (fixed or variable)
- Loan term
- Monthly payment
- Dealer fees (solar loans often include 15-30% fees baked into the system price — ask explicitly)
- Your ITC eligibility (honest assessment, not an assumption)

A company that emphasizes a low monthly payment while obscuring the total cost over the loan term is not being transparent. The comparison that matters is total cost of ownership versus total cost of grid electricity over the same period.

### 5. Honest ITC Guidance

The federal Investment Tax Credit — currently 30% of system cost — is a significant incentive. However, most Puerto Rico residents do not pay federal income tax on Puerto Rico-sourced income. No federal tax liability means the credit is worth zero dollars to you, regardless of what a salesperson claims.

The best solar companies in Puerto Rico will assess your specific tax situation upfront and tell you honestly whether you qualify. If you earn U.S.-sourced income — federal employment, military service, certain mainland remote work or pensions — and file a federal return with tax liability, you may be eligible. A tax professional, not a solar salesperson, should confirm.

Any company that builds the 30% ITC into your quote without verifying your eligibility is either careless or deliberately misleading. Either way, walk away.

### 6. Equipment Brands and Quality

Ask specifically which panel, inverter, and battery brands a company uses. Tier-1 panel manufacturers — such as Hanwha Q Cells, REC, and Canadian Solar — have proven long-term performance records. Leading inverter brands include Enphase (microinverters) and SolarEdge (string inverters with optimizers). For batteries, Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ Battery, and Franklin WholHome are established names with strong warranty programs.

Be wary of installers who are vague about equipment, use unknown brands, or push proprietary equipment you cannot research independently.

### 7. Track Record and Reviews

A company that has been installing solar in Puerto Rico for more than a year should have verifiable Google reviews, customer references, and a portfolio of completed projects. Look for patterns in reviews — consistent praise for communication, workmanship, and follow-through is a strong signal. Recurring complaints about delayed timelines, poor communication, or warranty runarounds are warnings you should take seriously.

### 8. Years in Business (in Puerto Rico Specifically)

There is a difference between a company that has been operating nationwide for 15 years and a company that has been operating in Puerto Rico for 15 years. What matters is island-specific experience — navigating LUMA interconnection, understanding local building codes, handling permit processes, and maintaining service teams that can respond when a homeowner needs help.

## Red Flags to Avoid When Choosing a Solar Installer

Some warning signs should immediately disqualify a solar company from your consideration. These are the most common red flags in Puerto Rico's 2026 market.

### Promising the ITC to Ineligible Customers

This is the single most common deceptive practice in Puerto Rico solar sales. If a company guarantees you a 30% federal tax credit without asking detailed questions about your income sources and federal tax liability, they are either uninformed or dishonest. Both are disqualifying.

### High-Pressure Door-to-Door Tactics

Solar is a 25-year investment. Any salesperson who pressures you to sign today, claims a price is "only available right now," or creates artificial urgency is prioritizing their commission over your interests. Legitimate companies give you time to review proposals, compare quotes, and consult with anyone you trust.

### No Local Office or Permanent Presence

A company with no office, no warehouse, and no permanent staff on the island cannot provide the kind of long-term support a solar installation demands. If they are operating from the mainland with temporary crews, ask yourself: who will be here in year five when something needs attention?

### Unusually Low Pricing

If one quote comes in dramatically below the others, investigate why. Possible explanations include lower-quality equipment, subcontracted labor, missing components (like a battery you assumed was included), or a bait-and-switch where the price increases after you sign. The cheapest quote is almost never the best value over 25 years.

### Vague Contracts and Missing Details

If a proposal does not clearly specify equipment brands and models, warranty terms, total cost, financing details, and production estimates, it is incomplete. A professional solar company provides all of this in writing before asking for your signature.

## Questions to Ask Every Solar Installer

These questions will reveal more about a company's quality and integrity than any marketing material. Ask every installer on your shortlist, and compare answers side by side.

1. **Are you licensed and insured to install solar in Puerto Rico?** Ask for documentation and verify it.

2. **Do you use in-house crews or subcontractors?** Accept nothing less than a direct, unequivocal answer.

3. **What exactly does your warranty cover?** Insist on a written warranty document that specifies panels, inverters, batteries, labor, and roof penetration coverage.

4. **How long have you operated in Puerto Rico, and will you still be here in 10 years?** Ask about the company's history, growth, and local infrastructure.

5. **Do I qualify for the federal ITC?** A company that answers "yes" without asking about your tax situation is one you should not trust with the rest of the process.

6. **What is my total cost — not just the monthly payment?** Demand the full financed cost including interest and all fees, not just a monthly figure that obscures the real number.

7. **What specific equipment will you install?** Get brand names, model numbers, and tier ratings for panels, inverters, and batteries.

8. **How do you handle permitting and LUMA interconnection?** A good company manages this entire process and can give you a realistic timeline.

9. **What is the estimated annual production for my specific home?** This should be based on your actual roof, orientation, and shading — not a generic estimate.

10. **Can I speak with recent customers or see completed installations?** A confident company will gladly connect you with references. Hesitation is a red flag.

## The Importance of Local Presence in Puerto Rico

This point deserves its own section because it is uniquely important on the island.

Puerto Rico is not an easy market to serve. Shipping logistics are more complex and more expensive than on the mainland. Permitting and interconnection processes involve local authorities and LUMA-specific procedures. Hurricane season creates annual urgency for installations and service calls. And the homeowner base needs and deserves companies that are genuinely committed to the community — not operators treating the island as a temporary profit opportunity.

A solar company with a true local presence means:

- **A physical office and warehouse on the island** — real infrastructure, not a mailbox
- **Permanently employed local staff** — sales, installation, and service teams that live and work in Puerto Rico
- **Inventory on-island** — equipment stocked locally so installations and repairs are not delayed by mainland shipping
- **Relationships with local authorities** — experience navigating permitting bodies and LUMA interconnection
- **Community accountability** — a reputation built with neighbors, not strangers

When your system needs a service call in year seven or a warranty claim in year twelve, a company with permanent local operations can respond. A mainland-only company may have moved on to the next market entirely.

## Warranty Comparison Guide

Use this framework to compare warranty offers from any solar company in Puerto Rico.

| Warranty Element | What to Look For | Red Flag |
|-----------------|------------------|----------|
| Panel performance | 25 years, guaranteeing 80-85% output | Less than 25 years, or no degradation guarantee |
| Inverter | 12-25 years (manufacturer dependent) | Less than 12 years or no separate inverter warranty |
| Battery | 10-15 years covering capacity and cycles | Less than 10 years, or vague capacity guarantees |
| Workmanship/labor | 10-25 years covering the installation itself | Not included, or only 1-5 years |
| Roof penetration | 10-25 years guaranteeing no leaks from mounts | Not mentioned at all |
| Labor included | Repair labor covered at no extra cost | Warranty covers parts but charges for labor |
| Company backing | Financially stable company with local presence | Startup with no track record or mainland-only ops |

A warranty is only as strong as the company backing it. The best warranty on paper is worthless if the company issuing it does not exist when you need to use it. Prioritize companies with financial stability, permanent Puerto Rico operations, and a track record measured in years, not months.

### Use Cost Per Watt and Cost Per kWh as Normalizing Metrics

**Cost per watt** (total price divided by system size in watts) normalizes quotes for different system sizes. In Puerto Rico, expect $2.50-$3.50 per watt for a quality installation in 2026.

**Projected cost per kWh over 25 years** (total cost divided by total estimated lifetime production) lets you compare the true cost of solar electricity against what you would pay LUMA over the same period. This is the ultimate apples-to-apples comparison.

### Do Not Default to the Cheapest Quote

The best value in solar is the lowest total cost of ownership over 25 years — not the lowest sticker price. A slightly more expensive system from a company with in-house crews, a comprehensive warranty, and a permanent local presence will almost always cost less over time than a bargain installation from a company that cuts corners on equipment, labor, or support.

A $3,000 discount that comes with subcontracted labor, a limited warranty, and no local service team is not a savings. It is a risk you will pay for later.

### How do I know if a solar company in Puerto Rico is legitimate?

Verify their contractor license through Puerto Rico's licensing authorities, confirm they carry general liability and workers' compensation insurance, check their Google reviews for consistent patterns of quality, confirm they have a physical office and warehouse on the island, and ask for references from recent installations. A legitimate company will provide all of this documentation without hesitation.

### Do most Puerto Rico residents qualify for the 30% federal solar tax credit?

No. Most Puerto Rico residents do not pay federal income tax on Puerto Rico-sourced income, which means the 30% federal Investment Tax Credit is worth zero dollars to them. However, if you earn U.S.-sourced income — from federal employment, military service, mainland remote work, or certain pensions — and file a federal return with tax liability, you may be eligible. Always confirm with a tax professional, and be cautious of any solar company that assumes your eligibility without asking.

### Why does it matter if a solar company uses in-house crews or subcontractors?

In-house crews are directly employed, trained, and managed by the solar company, which means the company controls the quality of every installation and is directly accountable for the results. Subcontracted crews work for third parties, and the solar company has limited control over their training, standards, or long-term availability. When a warranty issue arises years later, a subcontractor may no longer be associated with the company — leaving you stuck between two parties pointing fingers at each other.

### How many quotes should I get before choosing a solar company in Puerto Rico?
